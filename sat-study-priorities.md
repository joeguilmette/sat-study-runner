## Buckets

### Standard English Conventions: punctuation, titles, and modifier placement

- Priority: study first
- Misses: 3
- Time to improve: low
- Why it matters: Three misses in a rule-based area. This is the fastest bucket to tighten under time pressure.
- Questions: Module 1 Q17, Module 2 Q15, Module 2 Q19
- Pattern observed: The misses came from sentence-structure and punctuation decisions, not from misunderstanding passage meaning.
- Subskills to review:
  - Use commas to set off nonessential modifiers or interrupting phrases.
  - Make sure an opening modifier clearly attaches to the noun that follows.
  - Do not insert commas between a professional title and a name when the title is restrictive.
- Study actions:
  - Review nonessential phrase commas, title-name punctuation, and modifier placement.
  - Practice rewriting each sentence by identifying the core subject-verb first, then add modifiers back.
  - When stuck, strip away extras and ask what punctuation keeps one grammatical sentence.

### Craft and Structure: word-in-context and precision vocabulary

- Priority: study first
- Misses: 5
- Time to improve: medium
- Why it matters: Five misses, the largest cluster. Fixing this reduces repeat misses quickly because the question form is highly consistent.
- Questions: Module 1 Q3, Module 1 Q4, Module 2 Q1, Module 2 Q2, Module 2 Q3
- Pattern observed: The incorrect choices were plausible in topic or tone but did not match the sentence logic precisely enough.
- Subskills to review:
  - Predict the missing idea before looking at the options.
  - Use contrast and cause-effect clues to determine tone and direction.
  - Choose the word that is logically exact, not just generally related to the topic.
- Study actions:
  - Cover the choices and say your own word or phrase first.
  - Mark clue words such as although, but, thus, and examples that reveal whether the blank should be positive, negative, broad, narrow, stable, flexible, and so on.
  - Eliminate answers that fit the subject matter but do not fit the actual relationship in the sentence.

### Craft and Structure: passage structure, sentence function, and author purpose

- Priority: study first
- Misses: 4
- Time to improve: medium
- Why it matters: Four misses in a recurring question family. These are common on the test and improve with a repeatable annotation habit.
- Questions: Module 1 Q5, Module 1 Q6, Module 2 Q5, Module 2 Q6
- Pattern observed: The wrong answers tended to overstate motive, consequences, or contrast instead of naming the exact role played by the sentence or passage.
- Subskills to review:
  - Summarize each sentence or paragraph by its job, not its topic.
  - Separate what the text says from what the answer choice adds or exaggerates.
  - For underlined-function questions, ask why the author included that part right there.
- Study actions:
  - Annotate each paragraph with a short label such as problem, evidence, result, comparison, or qualification.
  - On function questions, finish the sentence: This part is here to ____.
  - Reject answers that sound interpretive or dramatic if the passage only supports a narrower role.

### Information and Ideas: tables, charts, and quantitative comparison

- Priority: study next
- Misses: 2
- Time to improve: low
- Why it matters: Only two misses, but these are formulaic and often among the easiest points to recover quickly.
- Questions: Module 1 Q13, Module 2 Q12
- Pattern observed: The misses came from choosing a claim that sounded scientific but did not exactly match the table values or stated relationship.
- Subskills to review:
  - Translate the claim into a precise data check before reading the answers.
  - Track min versus max, exact equality, and direct comparisons across rows.
  - Match every claim in an answer choice to a visible number or table relationship.
- Study actions:
  - Write the key relationship in plain English before choosing: softer than, equal to, shortest, same period, and so on.
  - Check every comparative word in the answer against the table one by one.
  - Prefer the answer that is fully supported, even if another sounds more interesting.

### Expression of Ideas: transitions and notes-to-sentence synthesis

- Priority: study next
- Misses: 2
- Time to improve: low
- Why it matters: Two misses, but both are skill-pattern questions that are usually quick to improve once the task is framed correctly.
- Questions: Module 1 Q23, Module 1 Q27
- Pattern observed: The incorrect answers were related to the subject matter but did not serve the author’s specific goal or logical transition.
- Subskills to review:
  - Identify the logical relationship before evaluating transition words.
  - Use the stated writing goal to decide which note is relevant.
  - Prefer the choice that accomplishes the exact rhetorical task, not just one that mentions the topic.
- Study actions:
  - Before looking at choices, label the blank as contrast, support, cause, result, or concession.
  - For notes questions, underline the goal and discard notes that are interesting but off-goal.
  - Choose the answer that does the job most directly, even if another includes more detail.

### Information and Ideas: inference, main idea, and evidence application

- Priority: study next
- Misses: 4
- Time to improve: medium-high
- Why it matters: Four misses here, but this bucket is less formulaic than grammar or table questions. It matters, but it usually takes a little longer to sharpen.
- Questions: Module 2 Q8, Module 2 Q9, Module 2 Q11, Module 2 Q13
- Pattern observed: The wrong choices were plausible but either added a new claim, shifted the emphasis, or captured only part of the evidence.
- Subskills to review:
  - Base every choice on explicit support from the passage, not a plausible outside idea.
  - For main idea questions, choose the answer that captures the passage’s center of gravity.
  - For illustration questions, match the example to the exact finding or claim.
- Study actions:
  - Paraphrase the passage claim in one sentence before reading the answer choices.
  - Use a strict support test: where is that claim in the text?
  - Reject answers that add motive, certainty, or scope beyond what the passage states.

### Craft and Structure: paired-text response and disagreement

- Priority: if time remains
- Misses: 2
- Time to improve: medium
- Why it matters: Two misses. This is still worth covering, but the question type is narrower and a bit slower to improve than the buckets above.
- Questions: Module 1 Q7, Module 2 Q7
- Pattern observed: The misses came from selecting an answer on the right topic but not on the precise point where the texts diverged.
- Subskills to review:
  - Identify Text 1 claim and Text 2 qualification or challenge separately.
  - Turn the disagreement into a yes-no question or a single sentence contrast.
  - Focus on the exact axis of disagreement, not the broader topic.
- Study actions:
  - Make a quick T-chart: Text 1 says ___; Text 2 says ___.
  - For disagreement questions, rewrite each answer as a yes-no issue and test whether both texts would answer it differently.
  - Ignore wording that sounds sophisticated if it is not the real dispute.
